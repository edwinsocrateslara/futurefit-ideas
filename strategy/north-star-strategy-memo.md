# North Star Strategy Memo

*May 10, 2026 | Confidential*

---

## Winning Aspiration

The goal is $33M ARR by 2031.

Our anchor metric is Lifetime Wages over Cost to Serve. The market is going to hire us to lift the top of that ratio (better jobs for more people) and to bring down the bottom (a faster, cheaper path to getting them there).

---

## Where To Play

We're choosing to play based off of an evaluation of the current market, how we think the market will evolve in 5-10 years, and our current strengths. The Market Map below is intentionally bounded. It reflects where we are placing our bets right now, not the full universe of what this technology could eventually address.

Zooming out for a moment: the macro opportunity is large. Across the US and Canada, millions of people move through career transitions every year, and the institutions supporting those transitions, whether government agencies, nonprofit intermediaries, community colleges, or employers, are broadly underserved by modern technology. AI is beginning to change what is possible in this space. We see that as a long-run tailwind.

**Where we are prioritizing now.** Government and nonprofit workforce buyers in the US and Canada. The organizing principle is simple: we compete where the goal is connecting people to work. That means state-level labor exchanges, Canadian employment service organizations, and regional workforce intermediaries. These buyers have the mandate, the budget, and the urgency.

**Where we are not playing.** K-12, higher education, post-secondary, and the employer/enterprise buyer segment are out of scope for the time being. Not because the opportunity is absent, but because each requires a materially different go-to-market motion and, even if our product has relevant capabilities, their needs are distinct enough that serving the segment well would likely meaningfully stretch our product. Outplacement, alternative sourcing, and workforce-oriented higher education systems remain on the long-range radar as natural adjacencies. None of these receive dedicated capacity this year.

**What we are monitoring.** Gov-adjacent private providers that deliver government-funded employment programs, such as Equus, are in scope where the mission aligns. Philanthropy and policy windows are also worth watching: they do not constitute a segment, but they generate one-off cash injections that open markets and extend revenue streams in ways that compound over time.

**The geographic frame.** Today, we are focused on the US and Canada. We'll continue to monitor the broader global landscape in this space.

**Why this discipline matters.** Our Government and NPO SAM in 2031 is estimated at roughly $400 million USD. Capturing 3 to 4 percent of that, through 8 to 10 US state-level contracts at average ACVs of $500,000 to $750,000, 6 to 8 large Canadian organizations at WCG and Bruce County scale, and 12 to 15 regional intermediaries such as TalentFirst, Colorado Thrives, and SEMI Foundation, gets us to approximately half of our $30 million ARR target by FY2031. That is a defensible position from a standing start.

This map will evolve. As we scale, as the market matures, and as we develop the organizational capacity to run more complex motions, we will take on more of it. But we will do that deliberately, with the right team and the right infrastructure behind it, not by spreading thin in pursuit of TAM.

---

It's imperative that we identify and pursue specific funding streams and policies that align with our strategy. Leveraging these will help us get a foothold to opportunities that can grow over time into LX/LMs. Keeping a pulse on the political, economic, and funding landscape will help us guide us to our North Star goal. We will need to be reactive to big changes (think Covid-era shifts, current discourse around AI displacement) and also help shape the market's understanding of these shifts to define and capitalize on the funding streams that result from landscape changes. Our customers also benefit from our help finding them funding and we benefit from knowing where the low-hanging fruit is. That said, we don't believe that hyper focusing or narrowing our focus to one or two policies (Workforce Pell, for example) will help.

## How to Win

We win on three things, and the three reinforce each other.

The first is **distribution**. We don't beat bigger competitors on individual features. We beat them because our customers trust our brand based on the breadth of problems we solve and the reach and scale of our work. The deals we lose, we lose to "you're not big enough" or "have you done this at scale before?" The work is to keep turning every stakeholder win into the reference that opens the expansion within that account or the door to the next customer.

The second is **the engine**. We're building a transition graph: a real, detailed map of who has moved from A to B in the workforce, by sector and geography and demographics and behavior, layered with the orchestration that actually moves a person through it. The graph tells you what someone should do next. The orchestration helps them do it. Nobody else is building that combination for our buyers, and that combination is the thing we want to own. We'll invest in a better front-end features in Pathways to hold the line on our distribution moat: governments still ask for it and ours is not as good as it should be. We know this internally though: The engine is the long-term prize.

(Build effort has historically been more concentrated at the surface, resulting in a UI that has grown broader and more complex across portals, without a commensurate investment in the data underneath it. We're correcting the ratio. The shift is toward depth: stronger data infrastructure, tighter feedback loops, and a front end that simplifies rather than expands — a chat-first interface starting with the job seeker, decreasing the surface area sitting on top of increasing data power. The outcome is a better learning engine while creating a better front door user experience, achieved by doing less on top, not more.)

The third is **proximity to dollars**. Today that means knowing how money flows through our buyers well enough to help them figure out how to pay us. In one year it means we more directly tie data and reporting to funding. In three years it means a pilot or two where the funding actually flows through us, and we are paid to spend their dollars wisely inside a complicated ecosystem rather than to solve a technical problem.

## Must-Have Capabilities

Four differentiators to be the best at:

1. **A transition graph.** The data asset underneath everything: who moves from A to B, by sector, geography, demographics, and behavior. Necessary but not sufficient on its own.

2. **Orchestration toward outcomes.** Our solution profiles a person, routes them, intervenes, and follows through. This is where reps compound and where we get paid. Guidance only matters if it leads to measurably better outcomes.

3. **Enterprise-grade infrastructure.** The privacy, security, integrations, and technology-as-a-service approach that any state agency requires in order to buy. We already have it for this problem space. The work is to keep it sharp.

4. **A multi-stakeholder town square.** Surface area for administrators, employers, providers, and individuals, configurable to each customer's local theory of change.

Funding connection points create visible, local ROI. Prioritization is firm: capabilities for administrators first, capabilities for employers and providers second, staff a distant third. We only build employer or provider capability when an administrator asks for it.

Payments proximity is on the watchlist. It could become a real capability later if we lean into it as a commercial moat.

## Near-Term Capability Priorities / Build Focus for the Next Year

**Make data capture and workflows seamless to grow LX & ON SSM market share.** *[Infrastructure]*
Administrators need to see what's happening in their programs: event capture, PIRL-compliant reporting, and clean data flows between FutureFit and reporting and case management systems. The employer portal holds the same bar — onboarding, registration, and talent recommendations have to work without complaint. Case management integration is the highest-risk item: scope uncertain for MA, external dependencies real, and critical for Ontario.
*Commercial stake:* the unlock for a state to consider buying and what keeps them from churning. It's one play in how Ontario becomes more than one catchment.

**Build the tools for regional and industry intermediary town squares.** *[Town square + Infrastructure]*
Configuration has to hold each customer's local theory of change — Palette's provider ecosystem is not Colorado Thrive's setup. Administrators get primary focus; employers and providers get what administrators ask for. Configuring relationships across administrators, employers, and providers help automate local realities (e.g. employer demand signals should be visible to admins and providers; providers and employers with pre-existing relationships should have those relationships supported in the platform). Table stakes industry-specific skills and funding connection point gaps must be filled.
*Commercial stake:* A renewal and expansion driver. It's the second play in how Ontario becomes more than one catchment.

**Transform the talent experience from "matching" to "moving".** *[Transition graph + Orchestration]*
In the short-term, eliminate friction in job seeker registration. In parallel, shift the GTM & data focus from a technical "ontology / match" value to an experience that visibly and proactively moves users to the obvious next step. Automate full jobs to be done in response to a "how can I help?" front door for talent, and a UI that maintains our distribution moat for administrative personnel. Profile depth and inference come next — enough signal to form a theory of success for each person, with routing that's personalized, relevant, and segmented to the streams we already serve.
*Commercial stake:* where reps compound. Better UX means fewer lost deals and better outcomes, and outcomes are what we eventually get paid on.

**Build the progress and outcomes layer.** *[Transition graph + Orchestration]*
Build the strategy and begin enabling the mechanism that lets the graph learn what drives outcomes. Progress and outcomes views land in administrators' hands in a form they can act on as soon as they are available. At least one validated case study — first- or third-party data — confirms a job outcome we sourced, so we have evidence behind the story.
*Commercial stake:* Differentiation that no competitor can replicate quickly. It's also the precondition for any payments-proximity play down the road.

## Management Systems

The operating principle is bias to action, against a clear target board of desired capabilities.

Whether it's a feature request, a new deal, a partnership, a hire, or a piece of brand work, the question is the same: why are we doing this? The answer should map to the four capabilities and the buyers we've named. If it doesn't, we either pass or we say out loud that we're making a strategic decision to build or implement something customer-specific and clearly outline the logic relative to our capabilities, strategy, and buyers.

Sometimes this means that we build deep, narrow, and customer-specific to hold the distribution moat. That's ok.

Four things we hold to:

- **Balance the customer in front of us with the buyer overall.** The most recent piece of feedback is not automatically the most important one. However, we need to delight our customers. Listen hard, then filter against the strategy.
- **Listen to the ecosystem and the people doing the work.** Customers, policy, funders, competitors, the people doing the work day to day. We don't win this market without being in it.
- **Tier discipline.** Custom asks of any kind, build or services or commercial, are only on the table for customers we've already decided are central to the next five years.
- **When something falls between the lines,** where it is strategic for distribution but off the named path, it bubbles up for immediate and active discussion -> resolution.
