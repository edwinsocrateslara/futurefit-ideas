# FutureFit AI: Market Diagnosis Memo

Date: April 9, 2026     Status: v0.6 — revised draft, internal use only

## Purpose

This memo is the market-facing twin to the Product Diagnosis Memo. Where that document turned the mirror inward — on our product, our gaps, our existential technical questions — this one turns it outward. The goal is to map the external environment honestly: what's happening in the market, what it means for our customers and their stakeholders, and where FutureFit AI sits relative to opportunity and risk.

This is a read of market conditions intended to sharpen our strategic choices in the Q2 roadmap and beyond. It separates what's true from what we should do.

## Part 1: Changing Currents

Before assessing our position, it's worth naming what is fundamentally shifting in the workforce system - the broader currents creating system pressure. The specific winds in Part 2 are responses to a deeper system dynamic. Understanding the system change is what lets us know which policy winds are worth leaning with, and which will pull us in a counterproductive direction.

Our revenue base, traction, and market exposure spans both the U.S. and Canada. Those markets behave differently in funding structure, regional organization, and outcomes accountability norms, and in other ways operate similarly in the issues they are attempting to address. Where the distinction matters, it is called out.

**1. Education and workforce infrastructure was already misaligned with labor markets.** Employers need specific skills; institutions produce credentials that may or may not map to them. The consequences are compounding: in the U.S., college enrollment has declined over the past decade while students who do enroll are increasingly in expensive programs with poor labor market alignment. In Canada, the picture is structurally different but equally strained: total enrollment reached a record 2.3 million students in 2023/2024, driven heavily by international students (up over 380% at the college level over the past decade), while domestic student enrollment has declined. In both countries, funders are losing confidence in ROI and employers are filling critical roles through pipelines that bypass traditional credentialing entirely. Apprenticeships and work-based learning are gaining traction precisely because they close this gap directly. Many programs are still funded and delivered in silos, making it difficult to create clear routes from learner to worker even where employer demand is strong.

**2. Automation will rapidly erode jobs and pathways further.** Uncertainty about which jobs survive, transform, or disappear is creating demand for "transition infrastructure," systems that route workers from disrupted roles into stable or growing ones, or help them upskill rapidly. This affects both white-collar workers displaced from entry-level pathways and hands-on workers whose jobs are either surging (healthcare, maintenance and repair, semiconductor manufacturing, data center construction) or facing automation (manufacturing line work, lifting and loading, truck driving). The system was not built for rerouting at this scale or pace. In both Canada and the U.S., this challenge is compounded by regional and provincial variation: labor demand, sector strategy, and public funding are organized provincially or by state, making workforce transition as much a place-based coordination challenge as a skills one.

**3. The system cannot measure what's happening.** Outcomes data (eg what programs lead to what jobs, at what wage, with what consistency) does not flow reliably between job seekers, training providers, employers, and funders. This makes it nearly impossible to target funding, prove its efficacy, improve program quality, or build the employer buy-in that short-term credentials require to be trusted. Where performance management does exist, there is a separate question of whether we are measuring the right things and whether the overhead could be meaningfully reduced. In Canada, outcomes accountability is real but unevenly operationalized: in Employment Services, results are tied directly to funding; in other segments they function more as a strategic expectation than a consistently enforced requirement.

**4. "Who pays? Who makes what decisions?" are unresolved.** In both Canada and the U.S., funding and delivery are separated across governments, intermediaries, and service organizations. The buyer is structurally different from the end user and often different again from the party held accountable for outcomes. As workforce systems reorganize around outcomes accountability, the question of who makes decisions and who holds the budget is in flux. Is it workforce agencies? State higher education boards? Employers? Federal compliance offices? States? That ambiguity is both a risk and an opening.

**5. The workforce system is fragmented, and that fragmentation is everyone's problem.** Workforce development is not owned by a single agency or institution. Higher education, economic development, social services, and state and provincial governments all have a stake, and each operates in relative isolation. This fragmentation is not just an administrative inconvenience; it is the structural reason why clear learner-to-worker pathways are so difficult to build and why outcomes data does not flow. Any vendor or policy that tries to improve workforce outcomes will eventually have to reckon with the silo problem. So will we.

These currents are converging at an unusual moment. Wage record data has aspirations to become more longitudinal and linked, as evidenced by the U.S. BLS Wage Records Program and Workforce Pell reporting drivers, but there are few examples of states or organizations doing this well at the macro level - it's mostly micro-examples of niche players layering dashboards on top of publicly available data that are filling the gap for now. AI tools have proven capable of processing complex individual circumstances at scale. Job market volatility is creating genuine urgency for better career navigation. Philanthropic risk capital is available to fund approaches that public procurement processes cannot at precisely the moment when traditional funding streams are under pressure. The three near-term policy winds in Part 2 are specific attempts to address the consequences of this system failure. They give us near-term entry points, while the broader opportunity is to chart our course based on the underlying currents, not just the winds.

## Part 2: Three Near-Term Winds

Each of these is a specific, time-bound forcing function. Each also maps directly to a gap in our current product-customer portfolio.

**1. Workforce Pell and Pay for Performance Apprenticeships create an immediate demand signal for outcomes infrastructure**

Workforce Pell goes live July 1, 2026. For the first time, federal financial aid will flow to short-term programs, but only those that can demonstrate outcomes and employer alignment. It is the culmination of years of pressure on education infrastructure to prove labor market alignment. Every institution that wants Pell-eligible learners now has an incentive to build the employer alignment data loop the system has historically lacked. It could also become the mechanism that incentivizes employers to share information with higher education. States and providers that cannot report outcomes are structurally disadvantaged before the end of 2026; those that can report will have a durable funding advantage.

Pay for Performance Apprenticeships reinforce this signal from a different angle: funding flows based on demonstrated results, not seat time or enrollment. Several states - including Maine, Washington, and Indiana - have already implemented skills savings accounts that allow workers, employers, and government to contribute funds for upskilling and reskilling. These state-level models are being watched closely as templates for a potential federal program. Together, Workforce Pell and pay-for-performance structures represent a structural shift from input-based to outcome-based in how workforce funding is allocated, with direct consequences for each system that serves learners and workers.

**The near-term consequence: there is an active procurement window for systems that can help institutions and workforce agencies get outcomes-ready. If we are not actively scoping this by May, we are likely watching from the sideline.**

**2. From innovation to infrastructure planning: LERs and talent marketplaces in the US; integrated employment services in Canada**

The Alabama Talent Triad launched in 2024 connecting learners, institutions, and employers through verified digital credentials. In 14 months, nearly 132,000 workers demonstrated capabilities through Learning and Employment Records. The LER conversation at the federal level is no longer "should we do this," it is "who helps us implement this without a $40M multi-year build." That said, at least half of the workforce leaders we meet at the state and regional level remain LER-skeptical given this is a conversation that has been going on for years and had little demonstrable traction or impact.

Workforce Pell and talent marketplace mandates are converging policy and funding streams. States that want to capture Pell funding need the outcomes infrastructure that talent marketplaces could provide. States evaluating talent marketplace implementation have Workforce Pell as a compliance accelerant. The two are moving together, driven in part by a more tech- and data-forward federal administration focused on reducing duplication and waste. Most states and jurisdictions remain in evaluation or pilot mode for AI-enabled workforce tools — which means implementation friction, not capability, is the differentiator. The state buyer is not choosing the most sophisticated AI; they are choosing the vendor who can get them live without rebuilding their infrastructure.

In Canada, the equivalent opportunity appears through regional workforce platforms, sector-specific ecosystems, and provincially supported coordination efforts. The dynamics differ: Canadian provinces have more direct relationships between funding bodies and institutions, and the coordination challenge is as much political as technical. But the underlying need — verified outcomes data connecting learners to employers at scale — is the same.

Ontario's Integrated Employment Services (IES) transformation specifically is modernizing how employment services are delivered across the province, consolidating fragmented local services into a more coordinated, outcomes-accountable system and opening new contracts for providers who can demonstrate results at scale. Similar modernization efforts are underway or anticipated across other Canadian provinces, creating a window for providers already embedded in the Employment Services segment to expand their footprint.

WCG is already operating in this space as a leading Employment Services provider, and their expansion into Toronto-area IES contracts is a live signal of where Canadian public workforce funding is moving. For FutureFit, this is a near-term replication opportunity. We have a working model with WCG, outcomes accountability norms in this segment are high (results are directly tied to funding), and the provincial modernization wave creates procurement openings in jurisdictions where we do not yet operate.

Canada represents a market where the path from current traction to embedded infrastructure is more legible than in the U.S. The employment services segment may be our clearest replication candidate. WCG is Green. The segment pattern that looks like potential risk in the U.S. portfolio looks like an early signal in Canada.

**3. The high-demand sector gap is real, funded, and routing-infrastructure-poor**

The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law created approximately 345,000 new trade positions, with peak demand expected around 2027–2028. That's five experienced tradespeople retire for every two new entrants. Meanwhile, 42% of Gen Z adults are in or pursuing blue-collar and skilled trade jobs, meaning demand-side resistance is softening faster than supply-side infrastructure can respond.

The infrastructure gap extends beyond trades. Healthcare, advanced manufacturing, and clean energy face structurally similar problems: strong employer demand, weak credential-to-job routing, and no reliable outcome data to attract training investment. Young adults don't have reliable pathways connecting interest to credential to job, though renewed interest in CTE programs is the exception, not the norm. Employers don't have scalable pipelines. Training providers don't have verified outcome data to compete for funding. The routing problem is the shared constraint across all of these sectors, and it is the problem FutureFit's recommender and LX were built to solve.

### What's Working

**1. Outcomes visibility correlates with renewal confidence.** Connecticut and Palette Skills (both score 90, both with Clear/Reported outcomes) are our two strongest renewal accounts. This is not a coincidence.

**2. Integrated platform use is a retention buffer.** WCG and TalentFirst both score Green despite incomplete outcomes reporting because the platform is org-wide integrated, not pilot-limited. Depth of adoption buys time. It is not a substitute for outcomes.

**3. Secured funding protects accounts in the near-term.** Accounts with secured funding are predominantly Green. Accounts with expected/partial or no funding are disproportionately Yellow or Red, including Palette Skills, which scores Green on outcomes and champion sentiment but carries partial funding exposure. The funding cycle, not product quality, is driving a significant portion of our renewal risk.

### What's Not Working

**1. Five Red-risk accounts and meaningful uncertainty across seven more.** Bruce County, NOVAworks, WSP, FCCQ, and TechFluent all score 50. Eleven of 19 accounts fall into "Some/Believed" on outcomes, meaning the champion thinks the platform is helping, but neither we nor they can prove it. That posture is manageable until renewal, at which point value has to be demonstrated.

**2. Colorado Thrives is an account we need to get in front of.** High executive buy-in. Secured funding. Org-wide platform use. Zero outcomes reported. This account most directly exposes the gap the Product Diagnosis named: we are in deep but not delivering on the data loop. It should be a flagship. Instead it's Yellow. The core problem is two-part: first, job match quality and apply-on-platform rates are insufficient and users are defaulting to Indeed and LinkedIn rather than acting on FutureFit recommendations. Second, even where users are applying, measuring their outcomes through the hiring funnel is painful (see Appendix A for the current outcomes collection workflow and its constraints). Both problems need to be in progress before this account renews.

**3. "Some/Believed" is a liability that Workforce Pell will make worse.** Eleven accounts live in this category. As Pell creates funder reporting requirements that flow down to our customers' customers, "believed" outcomes become insufficient. The accounts where we are getting by on champion sentiment will face harder conversations at renewal.

**4. Repeatability is proven in delivery, not yet in go-to-market.** We have developed internal repeatability in how we deliver within segments. The constraint is that our delivery playbooks do not yet translate cleanly across customers, and are not consistently producing repeatability in how we sell and position. Variability in jurisdiction, funding, and operating model means each deployment still requires meaningful reconfiguration and each deal still requires bespoke scoping — limiting the efficiency gains that segment-level repeatability should provide.

**5. We are not capturing leading indicators that precede outcomes.** Even before we can report job placements, there are earlier signals we could be tracking and improving: jobs clicked, applications submitted, employer connections made. These conversion funnel metrics are both a leading indicator of eventual outcomes and a product quality signal in their own right. We are currently underinvested in making these visible internally and to customers. A related opportunity: partners such as Year Up already have outcome data on learners who flow through our customers' systems, but that data is not being shared with us. Structured data partnerships with intermediaries and providers could close the outcomes gap faster than building new employer integrations from scratch.

## Part 4: Open Market Questions

These are the market-level questions where the wrong answer will misalign our go-to-market for the next 6 months. They parallel the open strategic questions in the FutureFit AI: Product Diagnosis Memo.

**Question 1: Which segments and use cases are worth going deep on, and which are one-off bets?** We have customers across at least six segments: state workforce portals (CT, CO Thrives, NJ), sector consortia (SEMI, SMART USA), employer-sponsored upskilling (TalentFirst, NCN), employment services (WCG, Bruce County), local workforce boards (San Bernardino, Snohomish), and outplacement/transition (WSP). Our strongest accounts cluster in sector consortia and state portals; our Red-risk accounts cluster in employment services and outplacement in the U.S. Segment attractiveness also differs by geography: a sector consortium in Canada may have different decision-making dynamics, funding durability, and employer leverage than a similar-looking U.S. customer. Is the cluster pattern signal or execution variance? The answer shapes 2026 sales capacity and product investment.

**Question 2: Are we competing for the Workforce Pell wave, and what does our response look like?** The answer to whether we're competing is yes. The question is what competing requires of us in the next 60–90 days. We have a partial story: the recommender, the LX, the data pipeline work. We do not have a packaged "outcomes-readiness" offering that maps to Pell compliance. What would it take to have something credible in market by June?

**Question 3: What does a replicable state or jurisdictional win actually look like?** Connecticut is our most referenceable state account. But the CT success was primarily driven by staff-supported workflows: the portal functioned as a front door for referrals, while coaching, training enrollment, funding access, and outcome data collection all happened off-platform. If we want to replicate CT in NJ, MA, or translate that playbook to a Canadian province, we need to be honest about what was actually replicable versus what was bespoke. Were the off-platform dependencies a feature of CT specifically or a structural constraint we'll hit in every state deployment? Without that clarity, every new state opportunity will feel like a greenfield build.

**Question 4: How do we move customers from "Some/Believed" to "Clear/Reported" outcomes?** Eleven of 19 accounts are in the "Some/Believed" bucket. This is not a product, customer success and data infrastructure problem. The current outcomes collection process is a multi-step, largely manual workflow dependent on employer cooperation, legal agreements, and ATS configuration (see Appendix A). What is the shortest path from a current Yellow or Red account to defensible outcomes reporting? Is there a version of that path we can package and repeat? Is there a technical path that reduces our dependence on manual employer ATS reporting entirely?

**Question 5: How do we treat the Canadian market relative to the U.S., and what does that mean when we have to make trade-off calls?** Canada is not a secondary market. It is a strategically different one. The U.S. offers scale, but Canada offers a more coordinated, outcomes-driven system where we already have real traction, particularly in Integrated Employment Services. Canada is also a market where we have a realistic path to becoming a big fish in a smaller pond, embedded in system infrastructure, in a segment that is starting to look meaningfully replicable. The Employment Services modernization wave in Ontario and other provinces creates near-term procurement openings that build on existing WCG momentum. When product and go-to-market decisions create genuine U.S./Canada trade-offs, we need a clear frame for how to navigate them, not a default assumption that U.S. needs take precedence.

## Appendix A: Current Outcomes Collection Workflow — Constraints and Open Questions

The following documents the current end-to-end process for collecting employer-side outcomes data. It is included here to make the scope of the problem concrete. The workflow is the primary bottleneck between "Some/Believed" and "Clear/Reported" outcomes for most of our at-risk accounts.

**Current Workflow (10 Steps)**

1. CS trains admin on how to connect with employer partners (demo, UTM code/source query instructions).
2. Customer rep reaches out to employer partners to set up a conversation.
3. Customer has the conversation and asks employer to sign a data sharing agreement.
4. Data sharing agreement goes through employer legal team. This step consistently introduces delays.
5. Data sharing agreement is received.
6. Customer rep asks employer to configure UTM code/source query. The employer rep frequently does not know how and must locate an internal person or ATS vendor rep to assist.
7. Customer rep follows up monthly or quarterly for outcome data.
8. Employer generates a report based on source and shares it with the customer rep.
9. Customer compiles a report across employers, using email or name as a unique identifier to map to training program enrollment or referral partner data from the FutureFit profile data export.
10. Customer distributes outcomes report to ecosystem partners.

**Key Constraints**

- The entire workflow depends on employer willingness to engage, legal team availability, and ATS configurability — none of which FutureFit controls.
- Not all employer partners use our Employer Portal, meaning even a well-built outcome tracking feature within FFAI will not capture outcomes for employers operating exclusively within their own ATS.
- The UTM/source query approach is fragile and frequently breaks down at the employer configuration step.
- Colorado Thrives is a specific case study in this friction: employer partners struggled to set up source queries and UTM codes, creating a bottleneck that persists despite deep platform adoption and strong executive buy-in.

**Open Question**

Is there a path that reduces or eliminates dependence on manual employer ATS reporting? Options to explore include: direct integrations with major ATS platforms, structured data partnerships with intermediaries already holding outcome data (e.g. Year Up and similar workforce partners whose learners flow through our customers' systems), and outcome tracking triggered by job seeker actions within the FutureFit platform rather than employer-side reporting. Each has different build cost, data coverage, and dependency profiles and should be evaluated against the urgency of the "Some/Believed" renewal exposure.
