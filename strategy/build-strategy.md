# Build Strategy: Four Differentiators Toward 2031

## Core Differentiators

**A transition graph.** The data asset underneath everything: who moves from A to B, by sector, geography, demographics, and behavior. Necessary but not sufficient on its own.

**Orchestration toward outcomes.** Our solution profiles a person, routes them, intervenes, and follows through. This is where reps compound and where we get paid. Guidance only matters if it leads to measurably better outcomes.

**Enterprise-grade infrastructure.** The privacy, security, integrations, and technology as a service approach that any state agency requires in order to buy. We already have it for this problem space. The work is to keep it sharp.

**A multi-stakeholder town square.** Surface area for administrators, employers, providers, and individuals, configurable to each customer's local theory of change. Funding connection points create visible, local ROI. Prioritization is firm: administrators first, employers and providers second, staff a distant third. We only build employer or provider capability when an administrator asks for it.

Payments proximity is on the watchlist. It could become a real capability later if we lean into it as a commercial moat.

## Near Term Capability Priorities

### 1. Get the back end right for LXes and Ontario [Infrastructure]

Administrators need to see what's happening in their programs: event capture, PIRL-compliant reporting, and clean data flows between FutureFit and state and case management systems. The employer portal holds the same bar — onboarding, registration, and talent recommendations have to work without complaint. Case management integration is the highest-risk item: scope uncertain for MA, external dependencies real, and critical for Ontario.

Commercial stake: the unlock for a state to consider buying and what keeps them from churning. It's one play in how Ontario becomes more than one catchment.

### 2. Build the tools for regional and industry intermediary town squares [Town square + Infrastructure]

Configuration has to hold each customer's local theory of change — Ontario's three-stream logic is not Massachusetts's setup. Administrators get primary focus; employers and providers get what administrators ask for. Configuring relationships across administrators, employers, and providers help automate local realities (e.g. employer demand signals should be visible to admins and providers; providers and employers with pre-existing relationships should have those relationships supported in the platform). Table stakes industry-specific skills and funding connection point gaps must be filled.

Commercial stake: A renewal and expansion driver. It's the second play in how Ontario becomes more than one catchment.

### 3. Move the user experience from "how" to "wow" [Transition graph + Orchestration]

In the short-term, eliminate friction in job seeker registration. In parallel, shift the GTM & data focus from a technical "ontology / match" value to an experience that visibly and proactively moves users to the obvious next step. Automate full jobs to be done in response to a "how can I help?" front door. Profile depth and inference come next — enough signal to form a theory of success for each person, with routing that's personalized, relevant, and segmented to the streams we already serve.

Commercial stake: where reps compound. Better UX means fewer lost deals and better outcomes, and outcomes are what we eventually get paid on.

### 4. Build the progress and outcomes layer [Transition graph + Orchestration]

Build the strategy and begin enabling the mechanism that lets the graph learn what drives outcomes. Progress and outcomes views land in administrators' hands in a form they can act on as soon as they are available. At least one validated case study — first- or third-party data — confirms a job outcome we sourced, so we have evidence behind the story.

Commercial stake: Differentiation that no competitor can replicate quickly. It's also the precondition for any payments-proximity play down the road.
